THE VOICE

Prose · quote · inline emphasis · pairing with the house

The voice serif is Valet's reading and speaking voice — body prose, pull quotes, tagline copy, inline emphasis. It is not the display type (Bebas Neue holds that) and it is not UI chrome (DM Mono holds that). Its job is to carry sustained prose in a classical register rendered in contemporary execution.

Five candidates are loaded live below. Each is shown at the sizes it will actually be read, rendered in the brand's voice — not lorem ipsum, but prose from the Valet voice itself. Each is paired with Bebas Neue + DM Mono in a production-shape layout so the three-font system can be judged as a whole.

Banned
Fraunces (Type's voice), Cormorant, Crimson, Playfair, Newsreader, Lora, Syne, any other reflex serif
Required
Italic + regular minimum · classical bones · contemporary execution · Google Fonts available
Register
Leather-bound ledger, engraved brass plate. Classical but not nostalgic. Warm but restrained.
Pairing
Must coexist with Bebas Neue display and DM Mono UI without fighting either

EB Garamond is Georg Duffner's open-source revival of Claude Garamont's 16th-century originals — the Garamond that actually belongs to the classical tradition, not the 20th-century interpretations that simplified its warmth. The italic is a genuine cursive Garamond, not a slanted roman.

Strengths: strongest material-register match of the five. Carries the leather-bound ledger reference most literally. Reads warmly at body sizes. Italic has distinct voice, not just emphasis.

Concerns: Garamond is the most recognizable classical serif in Western typography — risk of feeling familiar in a way that reduces distinctiveness. Also has a higher tendency to feel nostalgic than contemporary.

Cardo is the scholar's typeface — designed by David Perry for classical, medieval, and linguistic research, with extensive Latin, Greek, and Hebrew character support. It is not a display face; it is a reading face shaped by academic typesetting traditions.

Strengths: most restrained of the classical set. Formal without feeling stiff. Unmistakably classical bones but without Garamond's familiarity. Strong at reading sizes. No trendy flourishes.

Concerns: only two weights (400 & 700). The tone can read academic — which could work for the classical-service register, or could tip toward coolness. The italic is understated, which is honest but less characterful than Garamond's.

Spectral was designed by Production Type (Paris) specifically for on-screen reading at long lengths. It carries classical serifs and clear contrast but has been tuned for the pixel grid — smaller apertures, optimized stems, a roman whose proportions are decidedly modern.

Strengths: most contemporary execution of the set. Reads very well at body sizes. The design was explicitly intended for the kind of long-form prose Valet's brand may produce. Seven weights with italics is generous.

Concerns: the refined, contemporary feel may undercut the material register. Spectral reads as designed for a website, where Cardo and EB Garamond read as descended from a book. That gap matters for the brief's leather-bound-ledger reference.

Source Serif is Adobe's open-source transitional, designed by Frank Grießhammer and released as a sibling to Source Sans. Version 4 introduced variable-font optical sizing — the proportions shift subtly across sizes, keeping the type comfortable from footnote to page title.

Strengths: most architectural of the five. Clean skeleton, no decorative flourishes, contemporary execution without being trendy. Works exceptionally well in body + emphasis + heading roles. Open-source, widely tested.

Concerns: reads as contemporary and neutral more than classical and warm. The restraint that is its virtue may undercut the brief's material register. Source Serif is the font of a well-designed technical document, not a bound portfolio.

Young Serif is a contemporary reinterpretation of classical forms by Bastien Sozoo, distributed by Velvetyne (Paris). It has chunky, geometric serifs and pronounced proportions — a distinctive personality rather than a neutral reading face.

Strengths: most distinctive character of the five. No other CustodyZero product uses anything close to this shape. The geometric chunk of the serifs feels architectural — classical but rendered as an object, which closely tracks the brief.

Concerns: single weight only — no italic. This is a hard limit. A voice serif with no italic means emphasis must come from other means (weight, color, spacing), which conflicts with prose register needs. Usable for display-adjacent contexts; not usable as a body voice serif without a paired sibling font.
