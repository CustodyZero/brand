THE MARK

Six directions · classical-attendance register · stroke-based geometry

The accent is locked at B·04 bronze — every candidate renders in #9D7E49. The icon is a 64 × 64 architectural mark, stroke-based, legible at 16 × 16 as a favicon. Per the design system, it must imply boundary, threshold, or containment — architectural, not illustrative. Per the Valet brief, it must carry presence, attendance, or continuity without resorting to service-worker iconography.

Permitted
Architectural geometry · strokes · thresholds · corners · rules · marks on ground · pairs · standards
Forbidden
Bells · keys · keyholes · trays · serving silver · locks · shields · surveillance eyes · anthropomorphic marks · sparkle iconography
Sibling clearance
Must not echo Archon's corner-brackets-and-node, Factory's verticals-and-threshold, Type's margin-and-line, or StationZero's squared-zero
Specifications
64 × 64 viewBox · stroke 2.5 · linecap square · transparent background · color #9D7E49
I·01 THE POST Principal & Attendant

Two vertical marks, unequal in height, anchored at the same baseline. The tall mark is the principal; the shorter mark is the attendant, at post, at hand, steady.

The register is relational — not a frame around empty space, not a threshold to cross, but a pair standing together. This is the form most literally matched to the Valet paradigm: a principal and an attendant, both present, both continuous.

At small sizes the silhouette reads as two vertical marks of unequal height — distinctive, unlike any existing house icon. The baseline alignment keeps the relationship legible even when individual strokes approach 1 px.

I·02 THE BINDING Bookbound · Record · Continuity

Three horizontal marks of varying length, stacked with equal vertical spacing. The form references the binding marks on a book spine — the visible evidence of pages gathered, held, enduring.

The register is accretive: a record that has been kept. For a product whose central promise is continuous memory, this is the most literal material reference.

At small sizes the three-bar stack collapses into a distinctive silhouette unlike any sibling icon. It risks reading as a "hamburger menu" at 16 px if the bar lengths equalize — the varied lengths must survive rasterization.

I·03 THE STANDARD Classical Column · Serif Capital

An I-form — vertical column with horizontal caps top and bottom. The silhouette of a serif capital, or a classical column abstracted to its essential axis.

The register is architectural permanence: a standing thing, structural, vertical. It holds. The form is among the oldest in Western visual vocabulary — the axis on which a building stands.

At small sizes the cap-column-cap silhouette reads cleanly. The risk is that it may read as a serif "I" letterform — which could suggest Type. Since Type's icon is explicitly about margin-and-line (not the letter I), this is register adjacency, not duplication.

I·04 THE SEAL Signature · Authority · Mark Hung

A horizontal rule spans the canvas. From it, a short vertical descends — a pendant, a ribbon hanging from a document, the mark of an applied seal.

The register is authenticity: a mark that says this is authorized, this is yours, this has been signed. Sovereign. The descending pendant is offset right of center — asymmetric, composed, considered.

At small sizes the form reads as a T-shape rotated, or as a line with a hanging tail. Distinct from all sibling icons. The asymmetry is legible down to 16 px as long as the pendant remains off-center.

I·05 THE NOTCH Marked Post · Survey Mark

A tall vertical from which a short horizontal extends rightward at mid-height. A post with a notch. A surveyor's mark. The exact point at which attendance is registered on a standing thing.

The register is indexed presence — not a pair, not a record, but a single mark that says here. It is also the simplest form in the candidate set: two lines.

At small sizes this is the candidate most likely to read as a letterform (a lowercase "t" or uppercase "F"). The offset horizontal is shorter than it would be for a letter, but at 16 px this distinction narrows. Strong at 64 px; closer call at favicon scale.

I·06 THE LEDGER Entry on a Line · Temporal Record

A horizontal rule spans the canvas — a timeline, a ledger line. A small filled square sits on it, offset right — an entry that has been made. A moment of presence, permanently recorded.

The register is time as substrate: the attendant's memory written on the line of the day. This is the candidate most closely aligned with the product's continuous memory with temporal awareness differentiator.

At small sizes the filled block-on-line stays legible as long as the block doesn't merge with the rule stroke. At 16 px the block compresses to 2 × 2 pixels — legible, but approaching the visual threshold. Could be refined upward in a later round.

Reading the strip: no candidate duplicates an existing grammar. I·01 The Post sits cleanest — a two-vertical relationship that echoes no sibling. I·02 The Binding (three horizontals) and I·03 The Standard (I-shape) are formally distinct from anything in the house. I·04 The Seal and I·05 The Notch use two-stroke compositions but at orientations unused elsewhere. I·06 The Ledger is the only candidate combining stroke + fill — closest in grammar to StationZero (stroke + line) but visually entirely different.
Decision format: name the preferred candidate (e.g. I·01), optionally note any geometric refinements (e.g. "I·01 but with the attendant shorter / further right / baseline shifted"). If nothing reads, request a second round with new directions. Once selected, the icon is rendered at production dimensions and committed alongside the wordmark generator.
