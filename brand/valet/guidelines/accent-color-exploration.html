Accent color exploration

VALET·STEWARD

Four candidates · classical service register

The accent color is shared between Valet and Steward — one decision, two wordmarks. The register is classical personal service: discrete, attentive, enduring. These four candidates were composed against the sibling-product accents (amber, blue, pencil, green, signal red) to ensure each reads as a distinct house member rather than a variant of an existing product. None is theatrical. The selected color must signal presence without announcement.

01 DEEP SLATE Cool · Formal
Hex
#3E5366
OKLCH

Material register
The steel-blue of an engraved brass plaque that has gone cool. Professional without military. The color of a well-tailored charcoal lapel caught in low indoor light.
Reads as

VALET Accent · on --dark
STEWARD Accent · on --dark

Deep Slate carries the discipline of professional formality without tipping into institutional coldness. It is the color of record — ink on cooled paper, the back of a leather-bound daybook. On dark it reads as considered: a color chosen by someone who does not need to announce themselves.

Its strongest claim is clearance: the chroma is low enough that the mark never competes with content, yet the hue is present enough to carry product identity in favicon and small-format use. This is the candidate that aligns most directly with presence without theatrics.

Old Bronze leans deepest into the classical material register. It reads as the tactile warmth of objects that have been handled — not the showroom polish of new metal. Against dark it carries the register most explicitly called out in the brief.

The risk to navigate is house amber (#D4880A). Bronze sits further toward brown, with lower chroma and a slightly redder hue — it is close enough to feel related to the house, far enough to not be amber. Whether that closeness reads as family resemblance or confusion is the question this plate must answer.

Inkwell is the most formal of the candidates. It is the color of record-keeping — continuity itself, rendered in a single hue. For a product whose central promise is continuity of memory, this is the most literal material match.

The concern is contrast: at this darkness on a near-black background, the accent begins to recede. The wordmark will read, but the mark's visibility at small sizes depends on the final icon form carrying enough shape. Evaluate this plate by whether the color holds its own against dark, or whether it asks the geometry to do all the work.

Patina Brass is the most distinctive candidate — the kind of color that reads as a choice. It cannot be mistaken for house amber, Factory green, or any modern-brand default. It is unmistakably the register called for in the brief: age, quiet, duty.

The risk is register-fatigue: patina-brass aesthetics are particular and, if overplayed elsewhere in the product (in UI surfaces, in copy tone), can tip the product toward costume. Selection of this accent would lock the rest of the brand into a disciplined contemporary execution — the color is classical; nothing else around it should be.

Selection constraints

Must clear
Each sibling accent must read as a distinct house member at favicon size (16×16).
Amber Archon Type Factory SZero
Must carry
Discrete · attentive · enduring. Classical service register. Works for both Valet and Steward — the same color, two names, one material language.
Must avoid
Purple in any form. Consumer-AI gradients. Neon or glow accents. Theatrics. Anything that reads as announcement.
Theme
Dark only. Near-black stack inherited from house. No light variant planned at launch.
Color space
Authored in OKLCH for perceptual uniformity; hex reference provided for production. Chroma held low — accents carry the register through hue, not saturation.
